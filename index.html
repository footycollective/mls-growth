INTRODUCTION

MLS: At the Inflection Point

Introduction

For decades, Major League Soccer has navigated the unique challenge of growing the game of soccer in a hyper-competitive entertainment market. Since its inception in 1993, the league has been fighting a battle on two fronts: jostling for domestic attention with the NFL, MLB, NBA, and NHL, while competing for global market share with more established, financially dominant leagues like the Premier League. In order to simply survive, the commissioner's office has taken calculated risks time and again, further solidifying the league's position as a legitimate disruptor to the American sports ecosystem.

When the founding members of Major League Soccer sat down and agreed to establish the league as a single-entity, there was no blueprint - MLS became the only professional soccer league in the world to operate in this manner. By doing so, the owners sacrificed the growth of individual clubs to protect the well being of the league as a whole. Then, there was the Great Contraction. In a move rarely seen in professional sports, the league office decided to fold two teams (the Miami Fusion and Tampa Bay Mutiny) just six years after launch. Admitting fault so early could have signaled major structural and financial problems to the media and its investors, but eliminating these weak links instead sparked a new wave of growth, allowing the remaining clubs to make a push for soccer-specific stadiums shortly after. In 2007 was the introduction of the Designated Player Rule and record signing of David Beckham. While this could have tainted the competitive balance and exposed the league's growing pains to a global soccer audience, it became a tremendous enabler for revenue growth through new sponsors and fans.

Now, the league enters a new era of opportunity. Will the next set of calculated risks hit the jackpot, or is the league playing on winner's tilt and putting too much on the line? In this analysis, we will explore the five pertinent factors that can define the growth, or demise, of MLS in the next decade.

The Perfect Wave: The World Cup Effect - Is the league prepared for the influx of attention drawn to soccer in America this summer, or will it waste this critical opportunity?

The Great Pivot: Aligning with the Global Calendar - Will mirroring the European schedule prove fruitful in the transfer market and help fill stadiums, or cause unnecessary disruption for broadcasters, fans, and players alike?

Money Moves: Restructuring the Apple TV Deal - Should the league have shortened its contract with Apple TV, or kept the peace of mind that comes with three additional years of contracted revenue? What options does MLS have after the 2028/29 season?

2 Leagues, One Dream: The USL Threat - Is Major League Soccer as dominant as it once was, or does the USL and its promotion/relegation system pose a serious threat to MLS's position atop the US soccer pyramid?

Doomsday Prepping: Life After Messi - How will the league rebound after losing its biggest star? How can they learn from Beckham, PSG, and Barcelona to reduce the impact?

THE PERFECT WAVE
THE WORLD CUP EFFECT

MLS: At the Inflection Point | The World Cup Effect

Executive Summary

The United States stands at the threshold of an unprecedented convergence. The 2026 FIFA World Cup, the largest in history at 48 teams and 104 matches, arrives in months, followed by the 2028 Summer Olympics in Los Angeles and the 2031 FIFA Women's World Cup on American soil. Taken together, these three events form a decade-long pipeline of global attention, capital, and cultural momentum aimed squarely at American soccer.

The central question is not whether the World Cup will boost soccer in America. Thirty years of evidence has settled that. The question is whether the infrastructure now exists to sustain that boost, and what MLS clubs, executives, and investors should be doing today to ensure it does.

The 1994 Blueprint: What the First World Cup Built

When the United States hosted the 1994 World Cup, professional outdoor soccer was essentially nonexistent. The NASL had collapsed a decade earlier; there were no top-flight clubs, no soccer-specific stadiums, and no credible path to sustainability. The tournament shattered every expectation - 3.58 million spectators across 52 matches, an average of 68,991 per game, still the highest in World Cup history. Tickets averaged $58 nominal ($106 in 2026 dollars). A country that supposedly didn't care about soccer had just packed stadiums better than any nation before it.

That summer's success launched MLS in 1996 with ten franchises and a deliberately conservative model; centralized contracts, salary controls, and limited spending designed to survive long enough for the culture to catch up. It worked. MLS has since grown into 30 franchises averaging $721 million in value, drawing over 11 million fans annually, and backed by a landmark Apple TV deal. The 2026 World Cup is not a beginning. It is an accelerant applied to systems that already exist and are already growing.

The Price of Access and MLS’s Response

The 2026 edition introduces dynamic pricing for the first time in World Cup history. Category 2 group stage tickets have risen from $45 in 1994 to a projected $433 in 2026, more than 860% nominally, roughly four times in real terms. FIFA’s official resale platform, permitting market-rate secondary sales with 15% fees on both sides, has effectively institutionalized scalping within the tournament’s own ecosystem. Over 150 million ticket requests were submitted in the most recent sales window alone.

This creates a structural tension for MLS. The World Cup’s conversion power has historically depended on in-stadium accessibility. Affordable tickets put millions of casual fans inside venues in 1994 and generated the visceral, lasting connection that television rarely replicates. If 2026 is experienced primarily through screens and fan festivals, that emotional pathway is weaker. MLS clubs are responding intelligently: Houston is promoting $99 family four-packs; San Diego FC and others are planning “first match on us” promotions targeting World Cup viewers immediately post-tournament. The message is deliberate - you may not have been able to afford the World Cup, but MLS is right here.

Host Cities vs. Non-Host Cities: Two Diverging Playbooks

Of MLS’s 30 teams, exactly half play in World Cup host markets and the data already reveals a meaningful divergence. Host city clubs hold a 10.9% attendance advantage, a 35.3% franchise value premium, and are spending nearly double on World Cup marketing ($2.0M vs. $1.1M per club). Conservative estimates place the ecosystem’s aggregate marketing spend north of $45 million, the greatest single-year mobilization in league history.

Three club models illustrate the range. The Houston Dynamo are doubling typical marketing spend and converting Shell Energy Stadium into a climate-controlled FIFA Fan Festival extension - building on 18% attendance growth already underway, not masking structural weakness. NYCFC is spending at least three times its single-season marketing record with a deliberate eye toward its new Queens stadium opening in 2027; their Fan Zone presence at the USTA Billie Jean King National Tennis Center puts the future stadium site in front of its future audience before it opens. Chicago Fire, a non-host club, offers the most replicable playbook: more than $2 million in local activations centered on Recess, a West Loop venue serving as a Fire-branded fan festival for the tournament’s duration.

“We’re putting up four giant video boards, we’re spending roughly $500K on the AV to make this the best sporting experience, soccer experience in Chicago.” — Dave Baldwin, Chicago Fire President of Business Operations

The logic is clear: capture fan data through activations, offer post-tournament ticket specials, and convert casual interest into season-ticket commitments before the emotional window closes. For non-host cities willing to invest, the ceiling is higher than it might appear.

The Calculus: Quantifying the Return on World Cup Investment

The $45 million aggregate spend demands more than strategic rationale. It demands a defensible ROI projection, and the math is very compelling.

MLS drew approximately 12 million fans in 2024. The 18-22% attendance lift observed after 1994 and 2006 suggests a realistic uplift of 2-2.5 million incremental attendees in the 2026-2027 window. At an average revenue per attendee of $65 to $75, that produces $130M to $187M in direct incremental gate revenue, a 2.9x to 4.2x multiple on the $45M investment before a single media dollar is counted.

The broadcast and commercial layer adds more value. The Apple TV deal includes viewership-tied performance bonuses a World Cup spike is positioned to trigger, and host-market sponsorship inventory commands 15-30% rate premium in high-visibility years. League-wide sponsorship revenue could reasonably add $45M to the 2027 cycle on moderate activation alone.

At the club level, an average side spending $1.5M to $2M needs roughly 3,000 to 4,000 incremental season-ticket equivalents to break even over three years. At Houston, where 18% attendance growth is already documented, that threshold is already in range.

The conservative MLS-wide ROI - gate revenue, sponsorship premiums, and broadcast bonuses combined - lands between $200M and $320M over a two-year window against the $45M outlay. It is the 1994 blueprint, repriced for a league thirty years more mature.

The Attendance Trajectory: Can 2026 Be the Tipping Point?

MLS attendance hit a new league record in 2024 with a per-match average above 23,000 and total season attendance approaching 12 million. The league’s scheduling strategy is equally deliberate: MLS goes dark for seven weeks from May 25 through July 16, returning with six high-profile rivalry matches timed around the World Cup semifinal rest days, coordinated with Fox and Apple TV to capture soccer-engaged audiences at peak attention. All 30 clubs play on both the Wednesday and Saturday following the July 19 final.

The strategic threshold is 25,000 average attendance per match - a figure that would place MLS alongside established North American sports properties. New stadium openings in New York, St. Louis, and San Diego, combined with World Cup-driven interest, lay the foundation for that next phase of growth. Sustaining it is the harder problem. Every World Cup since 2006 produced a temporary spike that proved difficult to convert into lasting gains. The difference in 2026 is that the product, media infrastructure, and club-level sophistication are all meaningfully better than they’ve ever been.

The World Cup Hangover: Navigating the Risks

The most commonly cited hangover mechanism internationally, new stadiums built at enormous cost and left without viable long-term operators, is largely neutralized in 2026. For the first time in 20 years, FIFA selected an entirely existing-venue tournament, sparing host cities the white elephant problem that undermined Brazil (2014) and South Africa (2010).

The more relevant American risks are subtler. Fan fatigue during the seven-week MLS hiatus is real: casual fans who disengage during the break may not fully re-engage, closing the window faster than anticipated. The pricing controversy also carries reputational risk. If the “soccer is for everyone” narrative was undercut by a tournament that demonstrably wasn’t, MLS’s positioning as the affordable alternative needs to be marketed aggressively and credibly. The 2028 Olympics and 2031 Women’s World Cup provide critical insurance. Rather than a single spike and fade, these three events create a rolling wave of global attention across a five-year period. If clubs build effective conversion funnels in 2026, the compounding effect could prove genuinely transformative.

Conclusion

The 1994 World Cup proved that American appetite for soccer was real. The 32 years since demonstrated that patient infrastructure-building, from MLS’s cautious launch, through the Designated Player era, to the Apple TV deal and the Messi signing, can convert a single summer’s excitement into a sustainable industry. None of that happened by accident.

The 2026 World Cup is not a starting point. It is an accelerant applied to systems that already work. The clubs investing in fan festivals, data pipelines, and long-term hospitality relationships understand what they’re actually doing: building infrastructure for a compounding asset, not chasing a one-time event. For MLS leadership, the conversion window following the July 19 final will be the most consequential fan acquisition opportunity in league history - and it will close faster than anyone expects.

With 2028 and 2031 still ahead, the momentum does not stop, it rolls forward. The question isn’t whether 2026 will reshape American soccer. It’s whether your organization is positioned to be reshaped by it, or to do the reshaping.

THE GREAT PIVOT
ALIGNING WITH THE GLOBAL CALENDAR

MLS: At the Inflection Point | The Great Pivot

Executive Summary

Major League Soccer’s decision to shift to a July–May calendar beginning in 2027 represents the most significant structural change in the league’s history, with implications spanning roster construction, transfer market participation, and domestic fan engagement. The move addresses longstanding inefficiencies created by operating out of sync with the global football calendar, and arrives alongside complementary roster policy changes that together signal a new phase of participation in the international transfer economy.

However, global ambitions carry domestic risk, and matchday revenue remains a more critical revenue pillar for MLS than for any other major North American sport. The proposed winter break, running from early December through early February, presents both the league’s greatest vulnerability and its most creative opportunity of the transition. Taken together, these changes raise important questions about whether MLS can build its place in the global game without compromising the domestic foundation that funds it.

Why the Calendar Shift Makes Sense

For years, MLS has operated on a parallel track to the global game. That misalignment created recurring inefficiencies. Summer signings often arrived midseason and required lengthy adaptation periods. Outbound sales were mistimed relative to peak liquidity windows. International breaks regularly disrupted competitive rhythm.

Moving to a July–May structure addresses many of those issues. Clubs will participate fully in the global summer transfer window. Integration of new players becomes cleaner. Outbound sales can occur at more optimal moments. International duty disruptions should be easier to manage within a synchronized framework.

Beyond mechanics, there is also a signaling component. Alignment positions MLS less as an outlier and more as a participant in the global football economy. If the league wants to function as a primary transfer market actor rather than a reactive buyer, this shift was a logical step.

Transfer Market Positioning

Over the past five years, MLS has occupied an interesting middle ground in the global transfer market. When we examine average transfer spending and revenue per club between 2021 and 2025, the league sits in a cluster alongside competitions like Liga MX and the Russian Premier Liga. Spending per club slightly exceeds income, which reflects a league that is still primarily importing talent rather than exporting it at scale. By comparison, leagues such as the Eredivisie or Liga Portugal generate significantly higher transfer income relative to spending through well established player development and export pipelines.

Part of this gap has historically been structural rather than competitive. A large portion of MLS player movement occurred through mechanisms that were effectively invisible to global transfer databases. Intraleague deals were often conducted using General Allocation Money (GAM), a form of internal league credit rather than actual cash. Because those transactions did not involve traditional transfer fees, they rarely appeared in global data sources like Transfermarkt. Beginning in 2025, the league introduced a major shift with the creation of Cash for Player Trades, often referred to informally as “Cashfers.” Clubs can now use unlimited out of pocket cash to acquire players from other MLS teams. The LA Galaxy’s $2.375 million purchase of João Klauss from St. Louis CITY SC is one early example of this new mechanism in action. As more of these transactions occur, MLS transfer activity will increasingly appear in global datasets, making the league’s internal market more visible and comparable to other competitions.

These rule changes also interact directly with the league’s internal roster economy. When MLS clubs sell players to external leagues or receive cash in trades, they are allowed to convert a portion of that revenue into General Allocation Money. Currently, up to $3 million per season can be converted into GAM, effectively turning transfer profits into additional salary cap flexibility. This creates a strong incentive for clubs to operate as exporters. Selling a developed player does not simply generate revenue. It also produces the allocation money needed to build deeper and more competitive rosters. Homegrown players are particularly valuable in this system because their sales generate what clubs view as “clean” allocation money that can be reinvested directly into the squad.

The shift to a summer to spring calendar beginning in 2027 strengthens this strategy. Most global transfer activity occurs during the summer window, when European and South American clubs are actively reshaping their rosters. Under the current MLS calendar, those windows arrive in the middle of the league’s competitive season, which creates complications for both buying and selling players. Aligning the league calendar with the global cycle allows MLS clubs to participate more naturally in these markets.

Taken together, these changes suggest that MLS is entering a new phase of participation in the global transfer economy. The league has spent the past decade importing talent and experimenting with roster mechanisms within a controlled financial structure. With the introduction of cash based trades, stronger incentives to export talent, and a calendar synchronized with the global market, MLS is positioning itself to move closer to the transfer profiles seen in leagues like the Eredivisie or the EFL Championship. The shift will not happen overnight, but the structural pieces are now in place for MLS clubs to compete more effectively in the international talent marketplace.

Attendance & Matchday Revenue Risk

While global alignment improves roster economics, matchday revenue remains locally sensitive. No matter how globalized Major League Soccer becomes, the experience for the domestic fan drives financial success. Per 2024 Sportico data, seating/suites comprises 39% of MLS’s revenue. This is a larger share than the MLB, NBA, and NFL. Gate performance matters.

Our 2025 attendance analysis highlights one potential area of concern. Out of the 16 MLS matches played with a kickoff temperature below 40 degrees Fahrenheit, 5 matches drew attendance that fell below 90 percent of the respective club’s season average. That share sits well above the league baseline across all temperature ranges. In other words, cold-weather matches were more likely to underperform relative to typical attendance levels.

It is important to note the relatively small sample size. Only 16 matches in the 2025 season were played below the 40-degree threshold, which means the results should be interpreted with some caution. Still, the pattern aligns with common expectations around fan behavior and weather sensitivity, particularly in northern markets where winter conditions can make outdoor sporting events less attractive.

Two matches from March 8, 2025 help illustrate how these conditions can influence turnout. Columbus hosted Houston at roughly 40 degrees with cloudy skies and drew about 2,000 fewer fans than its season average despite minimal competing events. That same evening, New England welcomed Philadelphia with a 36-degree kickoff and recorded its lowest home attendance of the season. These examples do not prove that weather alone drives attendance outcomes, but they reinforce the broader trend that colder conditions can create additional friction for matchday demand.

Under the proposed July–May calendar, regular season matches would be played in November, December, late February, and March. Unlike opening weekend or the playoffs, these fixtures will not carry the built-in urgency that naturally boosts attendance. Cold-weather markets could therefore see more games fall into the types of conditions where attendance becomes more sensitive to temperature.

The calendar shift does introduce a counterbalancing opportunity. A May playoff window reduces direct overlap with the NFL, which has historically dominated the fall sports landscape. Competing with the NBA and NHL postseason is not trivial, but warmer weather and a clearer seasonal narrative could provide MLS with a stronger stage for its most important matches. The league is effectively trading fall football congestion for a late spring window of opportunity. That exchange may prove beneficial, but it will require intentional scheduling and marketing support.

The proposed early December to early February break also introduces a new variable: sustained fan engagement during a multi-month pause.

This period will coincide with the height of the European season and the NFL playoffs. Without careful planning, MLS risks fading from the domestic conversation. However, the break also presents creative opportunities.

MLS could leverage this window for controlled global exhibitions, youth tournaments, transfer storytelling, and targeted media content that builds anticipation for the February restart. Clubs may use the period for warm-weather training camps, regional events, or supporter engagement initiatives. The league’s relationship with Apple TV becomes particularly important here. Original programming, documentary content, and offseason transaction coverage can help maintain visibility.

The break cannot simply be empty space. It must be an active part of the calendar strategy.

Conclusion

MLS stands at an inflection point unlike any in its thirty-year history. The calendar realignment, the introduction of cash-based trades, and the broader roster policy shifts are not isolated decisions. They form a coherent strategic logic aimed at repositioning the league as a genuine participant in the global football economy. The structural inefficiencies that long held MLS back from competing in international transfer markets are being dismantled, deliberately and at pace. But the league’s ambitions abroad will only be sustainable if the domestic foundation holds. Matchday revenue still anchors MLS finances more heavily than any other major North American sport, and cold-weather fixtures under the new calendar will test fan loyalty in ways the league has not previously encountered at scale. The winter break is not a rest period; it is a proving ground for whether MLS can sustain cultural relevance in an overcrowded media landscape without live product on the field. If the league executes well, the mid-2020s will be remembered as the moment MLS stopped playing catch-up with the global game and started shaping its own place within it.

MONEY MOVES
RESTRUCTURING MLS'S APPLE TV DEAL

MLS: At the Inflection Point | Money Moves

Executive Summary

Adjusting the Apple TV contract to a shortened timeline is risky, but shows the confidence the league maintains in the future of its product. There were four key changes made in the restructuring of the deal, some of them necessary operational shifts, others more strategic. Adjusting the annual deal value was an obvious adjustment resulting from a broader calendar alignment and creation of a shortened 2027/28 season. The other changes, shortening the contract length, removing the paywall, and simplifying the offerings, were more tactical. Given the timing of the contract renegotiation, MLS took advantage of the opportunity and implemented all of these in one go. It may be too early to tell, but we will pay attention to how this impacts viewership ratings and new fan acquisition over the course of the season. Looking ahead to 2029 at the conclusion of the contract, the league has a few options. While D2C may seem enticing, this does not match the north star initiatives of the league’s five year plan, and they should instead look to creator partnerships as unique ways to diversify their streaming offerings.

Introduction

Around the world, TV media rights are becoming the most significant revenue-driver for professional sports, with leagues such as the Premier League leading the charge with deals worth over $2.1b per year. The recent Apple TV contract restructuring indicates MLS intends to compete with the four major American professional sports leagues and the top soccer leagues internationally, but what the league does after the 2028/29 season will be just as impactful, if not more, than any other decision that comes their way.

Reasons for the Changes

Don Garber has taken many other actions during his tenure to legitimize MLS, including most recently changing the regular season schedule to better align with the international standard. The creation of a shortened season in the interim is the most logical reason for the Apple TV deal restructuring, as the number of matches played in the original 10 year timeframe has since changed. Fewer matches means less total viewership for the season, lower broadcast revenues, and increased variability in the payment schedule to the league and its clubs. The new schedule also opens the doors to heightened competition for fan attention at different stages of the season, which affects the value of each Apple TV timeslot.

Since the signing of the original deal in 2022, the American soccer market has also seen the arrival of Lionel Messi, a Men’s Club World Cup in 2025, a Men’s World Cup in June 2026, the LA28 Summer Olympics, and the 2031 Women’s World Cup – all of which are expected to serve as catalysts for a new era of soccer fans. Not only does this drive more viewers to the platform, but first-time fans also bring a desire for a simplified viewing experience which the original Apple TV+ and MLS Season Pass failed to provide.

What Changed?

The restructuring of MLS’s Apple TV media rights deal can be summarized into four key changes:

  1. Shortening the Length of Contract — The original ten-year deal was truncated by over three years to conclude after the 2028/29 season, allowing MLS to return to the media rights market sooner.
  2. Higher Annual Deal Value — Apple will pay $275m for the 2027/28 and 2028/29 campaigns, meaning MLS will receive $50m more over the same period than it would have had the contract not been shortened. For the interim ‘mini-season’, the league will receive $107.5m.
  3. Removal of the MLS Season Pass Paywall — Starting in 2026, the standalone MLS Season Pass has been discontinued, and all matches are now included in the standard Apple TV+ subscription at no extra cost. This move aims to lower the barrier for entry and expose the league to Apple’s much larger base of tens of millions of global subscribers.
  4. Streamlining of MLS Offerings — The two parties reversed their decision to host commentators off-site, placing them back in the broadcast booth for a more authentic experience. The league is also discontinuing underperforming segments, such as the Spanish-language version of the MLS 360 whip-around show after data showed that 90% of the audience watched the English version.

What Does This Mean for the League?

MLS is betting on itself, and its decision to shorten the length of its contract with Apple TV tells us the league believes its product is only going to increase in value over the next 6 years. This is risky, but has the potential to pay off massively as we enter a highly impressionable era of U.S. Soccer.

The league is sacrificing 3.5 additional years of guaranteed revenue, which happen to be the 3.5 years after Lionel Messi’s contract expires. If Messi were to leave, broadcasters would lose a significant asset drawing both domestic and international attention, potentially decreasing the annual value of the deal. The league must view the other factors as more impactful, driving an overall increase in viewership even without its most valuable player.

The simplification of the offering tells us MLS is confident in its core fanbase and is willing to sacrifice the $100 per fan from the MLS Season Pass to bring in a new wave of casual supporters. If MLS is able to convert these viewers into match-attending, kit-buying, super fans, there is potential for something like the MLS Season Pass to return in the next few years.

Now a common trend we are seeing around the sporting ecosystem is D2C broadcasting, meaning leagues and teams are internalizing streaming operations to capture 100% of the income. While this may work for established leagues like the Premier League, which is testing direct-to-consumer streaming in Singapore, we do not see this as a wise option for MLS following the expiration of its contract in 2029. The timeline is too soon to convert new fans into core fans, and the overhead to build such a streaming platform is costly and time consuming. We believe the league should continue to partner with a reputable provider for the immediate future, and reconsider this option after the 2031 Women’s World Cup.

Along with D2C distribution, leagues are leveraging creator channels to reach new audiences. For example, Mark Goldbridge’s “That’s Football” YouTube channel inked a contract with the Bundesliga to livestream 20 Friday night matches to his 1.3m+ subscribers throughout the 2025/26 season. For a brand trying to attract an audience in a new, foreign market, this move makes sense. It does two things precisely well: it meets the target audience where they are, and it establishes credibility by attaching the Bundesliga name to a well respected figure in the industry. For a league like MLS that is attempting to build followership in a similar way, this presents an intriguing opportunity. By connecting with creators in target markets, MLS can drive fan engagement and capitalize on synergies now available following the schedule change (transfer market activity, international breaks, etc.).

TWO LEAGUES, ONE DREAM
THE USL THREAT

MLS: At the Inflection Point | The USL Threat

Executive Summary

North American soccer stands at a structural inflection point. The convergence of the 2026 FIFA Men’s World Cup, the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics, and the 2031 FIFA Women’s World Cup represents the largest sustained demand shock in U.S. soccer history. This analysis examines how the growth trajectory of the United Soccer League (USL), particularly its adoption of promotion and relegation, will impact Major League Soccer and the broader American soccer economy.

Our analysis yields three central findings:

  • Market saturation determines success. MLS and USL teams in “gap markets” (where they are the sole top-tier soccer provider) outperform those in saturated markets by 52% in average attendance. This finding challenges the assumption that bigger metros automatically produce bigger fanbases.
  • Competitive parity exists independent of league structure. Our Competitive Parity Index (CPI) analysis reveals that closed-system leagues like MLS achieve comparable competitive balance to open-pyramid leagues like the English Championship—though through fundamentally different mechanisms.
  • USL’s pro/rel adoption represents strategic differentiation, not direct competition. Rather than threatening MLS, USL’s structural changes position it to capture underserved markets and fan segments that MLS’s model cannot efficiently serve.

The implications are clear: American soccer’s growth will not come from leagues cannibalizing each other, but from strategic market segmentation where USL fills gaps that MLS leaves open. With this, these leagues can balance one another, and serve as partners that aid the holistic development of U.S. soccer.

Introduction: Two Philosophies, One Market

Professional soccer in North America exists at the intersection of two competing philosophies. On one side is the closed-league model, exemplified by Major League Soccer; designed to prioritize financial stability, parity, and centralized control. On the other is the open-pyramid model dominant globally, where promotion and relegation create competitive pressure, financial risk, and opportunity at every level of the table.

For decades, MLS has been the primary case study for American soccer economics. However, recent developments suggest that MLS is no longer the only economically relevant model worth analyzing. In March 2025, USL club owners voted to adopt promotion and relegation across a new three-tier structure, making USL the first U.S. sports league to embrace the global model. This decision, combined with USL’s announcement of a Division One league targeting launch by 2027, signals a fundamental shift in the American soccer landscape.

This section addresses a central question: How does USL’s growth and structural evolution support or threaten MLS’s trajectory? Will saturating similar markets allow for symbiotic growth, or will the rival league create friction and fragmentation?

The Gap Market Advantage

Our spatial analysis of 68 professional soccer teams across MLS, USL Championship, and USL League One reveals a striking pattern. Teams that operate as the sole top-tier soccer option in their market dramatically outperform those competing for attention in saturated markets.

The data is unambiguous:

MLS teams in gap markets average 52% higher attendance than those in overlap or saturated markets. The pattern holds across leagues. USL Championship’s top four attendance leaders, Sacramento Republic (10,500), New Mexico United (9,800), Louisville City (9,200), and Indy Eleven (9,100), all operate in markets without MLS presence.

Conversely, USL teams competing directly with MLS struggle. Miami FC averages just 1,100 fans per match, despite operating in a metro area of 6.15 million, because Inter Miami already commands that soccer market’s attention. Orange County SC draws 4,100 in the shadow of LA Galaxy and LAFC. The lesson is clear: market position matters more than market size.

Our analysis identifies 12 metropolitan areas with populations exceeding 500,000 that lack any MLS, USL Championship, or USL League One presence. Combined, these markets represent over 18.4 million potential fans.

This geographic white space represents USL’s clearest growth opportunity. Rather than competing head-to-head with MLS in New York or Los Angeles, USL can capture markets that MLS’s expansion model, which requires $300M+ franchise fees and major-market stadium deals, cannot efficiently serve.

Case Studies: What Happens When Markets Collide

The San Diego Precedent

San Diego Loyal SC operated successfully in the USL Championship from 2020-2023, averaging 5,800 fans and building genuine community connection. When MLS announced San Diego FC for 2025, Loyal’s ownership faced a binary choice: compete against a better-funded, higher-profile rival, or exit gracefully. They chose to exit. Owner Andrew Vassiliadis noted he was willing to invest long-term “if we were the only team in town,” but once MLS arrived with established academies and funding, “it just became very hard to digest.”

The St. Louis Collapse

Saint Louis FC folded in 2020 once MLS expansion was announced, well before the MLS franchise even kicked a ball. The USL owner, who became an MLS investor, determined that continuing “didn’t make sense” with MLS on the horizon. The pattern repeated in Austin, where Austin Bold FC relocated after 2021, unable to compete once Austin FC drew the city’s spotlight.

The Louisville Exception

Louisville City FC demonstrates what’s possible in an underserved market. With no NFL, NBA, MLB, or MLS competition, LouCity became a civic institution. They average over 10,000 fans per match, built a 15,000-seat soccer-specific stadium, and even spawned a sister NWSL franchise (Racing Louisville). The market proved capable of supporting two professional soccer teams because it wasn’t fighting for attention against existing top-tier sports.

The pattern is consistent: when MLS enters, USL retreats. But where MLS doesn’t go, USL can thrive. Will promotion and relegation concepts re-position this mindset?

Measuring Competitive Balance

A central argument for MLS’s closed-league model is that it produces competitive parity — the notion that any team can win in any given year. But does enforced parity actually generate more competitive balance than open systems? And if so, at what cost?

To answer this, we developed a Competitive Parity Index (CPI) that measures competitive balance across four leagues for reference: MLS, USL Championship, EFL (a promotion/relegation league), and Liga MX.

CPI Formula: CPI = (0.4 × Volatilitynorm) + (0.3 × (1 − Gini)) + (0.3 × Championship_Diversity)

Where:

  • Volatility = Average absolute position change year-over-year across all teams. Higher volatility indicates more competitive churn — teams rising and falling in the standings.
  • Gini Coefficient = Measure of inequality in final standings distribution. Lower Gini means more equal distribution of success.
  • Championship Diversity = Unique champions divided by total seasons. Higher diversity means titles aren’t concentrated among a few clubs.

The English Championship, a league with promotion and relegation but no salary cap, posts the highest competitive parity score. This challenges the American sports orthodoxy that parity requires centralized control. The Championship’s competitive balance emerges organically: relegated Premier League clubs bring resources but face pressure to bounce back; promoted clubs fight to survive; mid-table teams jostle for playoff spots. Every match carries consequences.

MLS scores respectably, but its parity is engineered rather than earned. The league’s strict salary cap forces champions to dismantle rosters after success. As analyst Taylor Twellman noted, when an MLS club wins, player performance bonuses count against next year’s cap, making it “difficult to navigate” and often compelling teams to trade key players. The LA Galaxy’s post-championship talent exodus exemplifies this dynamic. Parity is achieved, but at the cost of sustained excellence.

The Pro/Rel Question

The data complicates the pro/rel debate. Promotion and relegation doesn’t automatically produce more parity than closed systems. Liga MX, which lacks pro/rel, scores lowest despite operating in a competitive international market. The English Championship’s high score reflects the constant roster churn from clubs moving between divisions, which inflates positional volatility.

What pro/rel does provide is consequence at every table position. In MLS, finishing last means embarrassment but no existential threat — the franchise remains in the top division regardless. In the Championship, relegation means financial crisis, player departures, and years of rebuilding. That existential pressure changes organizational behavior.

USL’s adoption of pro/rel beginning in 2028 will provide a natural experiment. If the hypothesis holds, we should observe increased positional volatility and competitive intensity in USL divisions as clubs face real stakes for underperformance. But most importantly, real stakes and real emotions that perhaps, more fans will buy into. Thus, producing a formidable rivalry to MLS that will take viewers away in overlap/saturated markets leading to a drought in attendance in these MLS attendance markets, while the sports growth will climb holistically.

Historical Patterns

Attendance data from 2010-2024 reveals parallel growth trajectories across leagues, with both MLS and USL exhibiting V-shaped recovery from the COVID-19 pandemic.

MLS has grown from approximately 17,000 average attendance in 2010 to over 23,000 in 2024, a 38% increase over 14 years. More significantly, the league recovered to pre-pandemic levels by 2022 and exceeded them by 2023, demonstrating market resilience.

The USL Championship shows similar patterns at a smaller scale. Average attendance grew from approximately 3,800 in 2015 to 5,400 in 2024, with documented year-over-year increases of 30% from 2021 to 2022 and 14% into 2023 as fans returned post-COVID.

Growth Drivers

Several factors support continued growth:

  1. Infrastructure investment. New soccer-specific stadiums (St. Louis CityPark, Nashville GEODIS Park, San Diego Snapdragon) optimize the match-day experience and expand capacity.
  2. Expansion economics. New franchises in underserved markets (Charlotte, St. Louis) have demonstrated that “new team energy” drives initial attendance spikes, with Charlotte FC averaging over 33,000 in its early seasons.
  3. World Cup catalysis. The 2026 tournament brings 48 teams and 104 matches to North American soil. Media exposure, infrastructure investment, and cultural momentum will reach unprecedented levels.
  4. Demographic tailwinds. Hispanic Americans, the fastest-growing U.S. demographic, over-index on soccer interest. Youth participation continues rising, creating generational fan pipelines.

USL’s Differentiation Strategy

USL’s structural changes, including promotion/relegation, Division One aspirations, and geographic expansion into underserved markets represent strategic differentiation rather than direct MLS competition. The leagues are pursuing different segments of the American soccer market.

MLS targets: Major metros, high franchise fees ($300M+), marquee international signings, national media deals, stadium partnerships with municipal governments.

USL targets: Secondary markets, lower entry costs, community-rooted ownership, regional media, pathway-to-pro narratives.

This segmentation is economically rational. USL cannot out-spend MLS for Lionel Messi; MLS cannot efficiently serve Chattanooga or Burlington, Vermont. By occupying different ecological niches, both leagues can grow without zero-sum competition.

The Co-Existence Thesis

Evidence suggests American soccer can support multiple professional structures:

  1. Geographic complementarity. Our gap market analysis identifies 18.4M+ potential fans in metros without top-tier soccer. USL can serve these markets without threatening MLS territories.
  2. Fan segmentation. Some fans want global stars and ESPN broadcasts; others want community connection and accessible ticket prices. Different products for different preferences.
  3. Development pathway. A robust USL creates player development infrastructure that ultimately benefits MLS. FC Cincinnati and Minnesota United both graduated from USL to MLS, bringing established fanbases and operational experience.
  4. Competitive pressure. USL’s pro/rel adoption pressures MLS to evolve. Even without adopting pro/rel itself, MLS may loosen roster restrictions or expand more aggressively to maintain relevance.

Risks and Contingencies

The optimistic scenario assumes continued growth. Several risks warrant monitoring:

  1. Direct market conflicts. If USL Division One explicitly targets MLS markets, the collaborative dynamic shifts to destructive competition.
  2. Financial instability. Pro/rel introduces relegation risk that American investors may not tolerate. If relegated clubs fold rather than compete in lower divisions, the system fails.
  3. World Cup hangover. Major tournaments create temporary demand spikes. If leagues over-expand based on 2026-era enthusiasm, a post-tournament correction could prove painful.
  4. Media fragmentation. Soccer’s broadcast rights are scattered across ESPN, Apple TV, CBS, Paramount+, and league-specific platforms. This fragmentation limits casual viewership and cultural penetration.

Conclusion: Moving Toward a Multi-League Ecosystem

The American soccer market is not zero-sum. MLS and USL are not competing for a fixed pie; they are collectively expanding the pie by serving different markets, fan preferences, and structural philosophies.

Our analysis supports three actionable conclusions:

  • For MLS: Continue major-market consolidation but recognize that the closed-league model has natural limits. The 52% attendance advantage enjoyed by gap-market teams suggests that expansion into secondary markets could outperform additional franchises in saturated metros. San Diego’s entry makes strategic sense; a third New York-area team would not.
  • For USL: Double down on underserved markets. The Louisville and New Mexico models demonstrate that smaller markets, properly cultivated, can generate passionate fanbases and sustainable economics. Pro/rel provides narrative stakes that closed leagues cannot match.
  • For the sport: The 2026-2031 window represents a generational opportunity. Three major tournaments on American soil will drive unprecedented attention. The question is whether that attention converts to durable fandom. The answer depends on giving fans meaningful stakes, whether through the championship drama MLS provides or the existential jeopardy that pro/rel introduces. Different stakes for different fans, but stakes nonetheless.

The data suggests both models can thrive. MLS is drifting towards becoming more of “entertainment products” and the USL is going the other way, a more community based ritual. The future of American soccer is not MLS versus USL; it’s MLS and USL, serving complementary roles in a maturing ecosystem. The rising tide can lift all boats, provided each league stays in its lane.

What This Means for MLS Growth

The aggregate effect of these factors points toward sustained but structurally constrained MLS growth over the next decade. The league benefits from powerful tailwinds: World Cup-driven attention, demographic shifts favoring soccer, new stadium infrastructure, and expansion into proven gap markets. Our attendance projections suggest MLS could reach 27,000+ average attendance by 2030, a 15% increase from current levels and nearly double the league’s 2010 figures. However, the ceiling on that growth is defined by the league’s own structural choices. The closed-league model that enabled MLS’s stability now limits its upside. The 52% attendance premium enjoyed by gap-market teams reveals that MLS’s expansion strategy matters more than its spending power, yet the league continues pursuing saturated metros (a potential third New York franchise, for instance) rather than underserved regions where demand exceeds supply. Meanwhile, USL’s pro/rel adoption creates a competitive pressure MLS has never faced: a domestic rival offering something structurally different, not just financially inferior. If USL successfully converts the drama of promotion and relegation into sustained fan engagement, particularly in the markets MLS cannot efficiently serve, it will force a strategic reckoning in MLS boardrooms. The league may need to choose between its parity-first philosophy and its ambition to compete globally. Loosening salary cap restrictions could improve on-field quality and international competitiveness but would sacrifice the competitive balance that differentiates MLS from top-heavy European leagues. The coming years will reveal whether MLS’s model is a feature or a limitation; whether engineered parity is a sustainable competitive advantage or a ceiling that prevents the league from reaching the next tier of global relevance. The data suggests MLS will grow regardless, but the rate and ceiling of that growth depend on how the league responds to USL’s structural challenge, the World Cup’s temporary demand spike, and the fundamental question of whether American fans want stability or stakes.

DOOMSDAY PREPPING
LIFE AFTER MESSI

MLS: At the Inflection Point | Life After Messi

Executive Summary

As the MLS prepares for Messi’s eventual departure, it is critical that Don Garber develops a long-term strategy before it’s too late. Without Messi, the league will be affected in four primary revenue areas: sponsorship, media rights, jersey sales, and matchday. To best protect these streams of income, MLS should reflect on David Beckham’s tenure in Los Angeles and learn from clubs like FC Barcelona and PSG. The Beckham Clause is one and done, and the league instead gave Messi the chance to invest in a minority stake of Inter Miami, not the league as a whole. This shows that MLS has thought a few steps ahead, tailoring Messi’s contract with the end in mind. As for other clubs, PSG handled the change much better than Barcelona, as they took the necessary steps from a commercial and sporting standpoint to reduce the financial drop off after the Argentine’s exit. Inter Miami and MLS should replicate this and target long-term sponsorship deals that extend beyond the duration of Messi’s contract. Additionally, they should continue to invest in academy programs and youthful talent. By taking these steps, the league can place itself in an advantageous position, reducing the overall impact that comes with losing its star player.

Messi’s Immediate Impact

Lionel Messi’s impact on Major League Soccer is easy to see. Since his arrival from Paris Saint-Germain in 2023, Inter Miami not only won their first MLS Cup, but the club also boosted their valuation from $600 million to $1.35 billion, building a global Instagram following of 18 million fans along the way. With MLS’s single-entity structure, the Argentine’s tide lifts all boats. Sponsorship revenue across the league increased 13% in Messi’s first year, and average team valuations have grown from $571 million to $731 million, an 8.58% increase YoY. Soccer is becoming more popular as a whole, with league-wide attendance levels rising 4% in 2024 matches that did not involve Inter Miami, and youth involvement growing rapidly as well. In the three years after Messi’s arrival, youth soccer participation has surged nearly 42%, a stark contrast from the 9% seen in the three years prior. All of these factors indicate that Lionel Messi has been a welcomed catalyst for the game of soccer in North America. So while nobody wants to think about life without Leo in the US, this inevitability is something the league must consider and be prepared for.

Building the Foundation: Learning from David Beckham’s Reign

This level of growth is something MLS has seen once before, following the signing of David Beckham from Real Madrid in January 2007. At the time, MLS was recovering from a near financial disaster, being forced to dissolve two franchises including Supporter’s Shield champions, the Miami Fusion, in late 2001. Attendance was low and franchises were struggling to compete with the big four American leagues. Bringing David Beckham to LA was not only a last-stitch effort, but a pivotal moment that reshaped the fabric of MLS for years to come.

With Beckham came change, and none more important than the inception of the “Designated Player Rule”, which allowed clubs to exceed the $2 million salary cap for the first time in the league’s history. This rule has since been used to attract premier talent including the likes of Thierry Henry, Kaká, and most recently, Antoine Griezmann. While this drives media attention and ticket sales for well-positioned teams, not all clubs can afford to bring in a superstar. With Beckham’s signing, LA Galaxy became a force to be reckoned with, winning back to back MLS Cups in 2011 and 2012.

To better balance the competitive landscape for teams of all markets, the league has continued to modernize by introducing new variations of the Designated Player Rule. Firstly by increasing the DP limit to three players per roster in 2010, with a luxury tax for the third being distributed to the rest of the league. In 2012, it added the “Young Designated Player Rule” adjusting the cap hit to encourage the signing of young talent, like Miguel Almirón and Thiago Almada. “Target Allocation Money” was put in place in 2015 allowing clubs to buy down the contracts of players making just over the threshold so they were no longer listed as DPs. Then in 2021, MLS further incentivized youth development with the U22 initiative, so clubs could sign U22 players for unlimited transfer fees without breaking the DP threshold. Tying these all together, the league further flexed the roster restrictions by creating a “Choose Your Path” model in 2024, giving teams the choice of signing 3 DPs + 3 U22 Initiative players or 2 DPs + 4 U22 Initiative players for an additional $2 million in allocation money. Post-2026 World Cup, the league must balance two goals: maintaining a level playing field with tight roster restrictions, and letting teams spend enough to meet the rising fan demand.

While the Designated Player Rule is often referred to as “The Beckham Rule”, the “Beckham Clause” is just as innovative. As part of Beckham’s first contract with LA Galaxy, MLS opened the door for the Englishman to purchase an expansion franchise for just $25 million at the end of his playing career. At the time the contract was signed, $25 million was an average valuation, but by the time Inter Miami played their inaugural game in 2020, an expansion license cost prospective owners nearly $200 million. While in this circumstance, Don Garber’s gamble paid dividends and accelerated the growth of the league during one of most impressionable periods of soccer influence the country has ever seen, it is a risk the league is not willing to take again. As our analysis presents, with the increase in the number of USL franchises and fewer MLS-ready markets available, the league has not afforded this luxury to any other players — making the Beckham Clause a truly one of a kind opportunity.

Without replicating the Beckham Clause, the league had to come up with innovative new ways to negotiate a contract that would entice Messi to the states. On top of the lucrative piece of the Apple TV pie, David Beckham and MLS offered Messi the opportunity to purchase a minority stake in Inter Miami after his retirement. This offers a similar financial benefit to the player without impacting the structure of the league as a whole.

Studying Footprints: Learning from Barcelona and PSG’s Messi Breakups

Using data from the 2026 Deloitte Money League Report, we see that both Barcelona and PSG, Messi’s previous two clubs, experienced increases in revenue following his departure. While it is worth noting that other factors are at play here, such as the prevalence of the COVID 19 pandemic, the data deserves a deeper analysis into how these clubs prepared themselves for life after Messi — providing a blueprint that Inter Miami can look to replicate.

FC Barcelona

Many fans thought they’d never see the day Barcelona’s iconic figurehead left the club he had represented since age 13. After the club announced Messi was leaving in August 2021, sporting performance dropped, very much unlike the consistent success that fans of FC Barcelona had been used to during the Messi era. This can be attributed to a lack of true leadership on the pitch, and a loss of attacking identity. To avoid this, Inter Miami is starting to build a contingency plan early, symbolized by the signing of Rodrigo De Paul who was brought in at the peak of his prime off the back of a World Cup win with Argentina. The MLS club should continue to seek talent that supersedes Messi’s tenure.

Barcelona also experienced some financial challenges around the time of Messi’s exit, struggling to register key players and relying on veterans like Jordi Alba, Pique, and Sergio Busquets to reduce their salaries. The club overspent after the sale of Neymar, signing players like Phillippe Coutinho and Ousmane Dembele for exorbitant fees. The lack of revenue from falling out of the Champions League in the group stage and related COVID 19 related matchday losses led to a backoffice battle to keep the club functioning, and forced a major sell off of club rights for cash. While MLS’s salary caps and restrictions are in place to prevent these types of scenarios, Inter Miami must be strategic about the financing of expensive endeavors like the new $1 billion Freedom Park stadium. Even in the wake of a World Cup in the United States in 2026, Inter Miami should take conservative stances when forecasting for the years after Messi’s contract expires.

Paris Saint-Germain

PSG’s story is one of less hiccups and more immediate treasures. One critical factor in the commercial success of PSG is the length of their partnership contracts, carrying a sustainable global brand through different generations of players, including the arrival and departure of Ibrahimovic, Neymar, Messi, and Mbappe. PSG locked in a deal with Nike in 2019 that extends through 2032, and the Jumpman agreement was originally signed for 2018 through 2025, with the parties exercising a 2 year extension through 2027. In this case, the revision of the Apple TV deal indicates that MLS are trying to capitalize on Messi mania by restructuring the agreement to a higher dollar value and shorter timeline, one that more closely resembles Lionel Messi’s contract end date. The club are also signing sponsorship deals that close in 2028, including their sleeve deal with Lowe’s. This puts the club at risk of losing mass sponsorship revenue in the years following Messi’s departure, and that variability in revenue can lead to significant performance impacts on the pitch.

PSG also took advantage of the opportunity to shed its identity and rebrand to one focused on homegrown talent and youth development, without relying on foreign stars. This shift, under the unity of manager Luis Enrique and club ownership, brought Paris its first UEFA Champions League trophy just two years after Messi left for the US. If Inter Miami wants to continue its sporting success after the MVP moves on, it needs to invest in its academy now.

Our Prediction

Lionel Messi’s MLS cameo has proven to be fruitful for all parties involved. With him comes the public eye, dollar signs, and a tidal wave of fans. Without him, clubs are still able to find financial success by delivering a well-balanced sporting product and consistent brand.

We believe Inter Miami will continue to compete after King Leo’s eventual departure, as they have two reputable figures in their front office. One, a skilled operator who is able to navigate MLS roster constraints like Messi navigates the pitch, Director of Player Personnel, Meghan Cameron. The other, a celebrity owner in David Beckham, who is able to attract sponsorships and build popularity like no other. Additionally, there is the likely outcome that Messi maintains his involvement with the club as an investor after retirement. They also have a new stadium on the way which will deliver year-round commercial value, and a strong academy that has already produced some bright young talents like Benjamin Cremaschi who is currently on loan with an option to buy at Parma.

Now the question becomes, how will MLS fare after all of this? Let’s break down the numbers to see just how dependent the league is on their star player.

Sponsorships

One year after Messi left France, DAZN terminated their five-year domestic broadcast agreement. The media company cited “insufficient action by the league to combat piracy and mitigate the impact on subscriber numbers,” but the timing of it all begs the question: did DAZN no longer see the return on investment without the league’s top star?

According to the Deloitte Annual Review of Football Finance, from 2023/24 to 2024/25, Ligue 1’s total sponsorship revenue dropped from €2.551b to €2.4b, a projected 6% decrease followed by a 29% decrease the following season.

Now MLS faces a similar scenario on the horizon, with the current media rights deal expiring at roughly the same time as Lionel Messi’s contract in 2028. While MLS has other internal and external variables that suggest it will be safe, there is potential for history to repeat itself.

If we take a moderate approach, a comparable 6% drop in sponsorship revenue would hurt, but not destroy, MLS’s revenue forecast.

Messi’s Cut of the Apple TV Pie

A loss of the league MVP could bode well for the league as it looks to sign a new broadcast deal in 2028, mainly because one of the key revenue sharers would be out of contract. While the information is not shared publicly, we know that Lionel Messi receives a portion of the proceeds brought in by new Apple TV subscribers. While the MLS Season Pass is no longer paywalled, we can use this to estimate how much Messi is making per year on his deal with Apple.

Sources suggest the MLS Season Pass brought in 1m new subscribers per year. Knowing the MLS Season Pass costs $100 per year, this tells us the subscription is attributable to ~$100m per year. Assuming Messi’s contract follows an industry standard approach, he is making 15% off each new subscriber, which suggests a $15m payout to the player each year.

New deals mean new terms, and the league could be set to retain this $15m per year after Messi steps away.

Jersey Sales

Lionel Messi ranked second in player jerseys sold in the year 2025 with 1.2m sales, only trailing Lamine Yamal with 1.3m. Each replica jersey sells for approximately $130, bringing the total revenue for the year 2025 to ~$156,000,000. Using other deals as a baseline, Adidas retains a majority of this, sharing ~20% with Inter Miami and the league. Assuming ⅓ of clubs’ commercial revenue is shared with the league as part of the single-entity structure, we can estimate that MLS makes just over $10m per year on Messi jersey sales alone.

Without Messi’s global influence and consistent jersey sale revenue, MLS is set to take another minor hit to the balance sheet.

Matchday

Using a predictive model, we were able to estimate attendance per club over the 2025 season without Messi’s influence. By isolating Messi’s appearances, we compared the average attendance for matches with Messi and matches without. Then, we replaced the attendance for matches where Messi featured with the average attendance for that club in matches without him. Using Forbes’s 2025 revenues per MLS franchise, we quantified the financial impact for each club in the simulated season. The findings are summarized below:

  1. League-wide attendance only dropped 1.27% in the simulated 2025 campaign, signifying strong support from clubs regardless of who is on the pitch.
  2. There were three clubs that were “immune to Messi”, meaning their average attendance in matches without Messi was greater than or equal to their actual attendance. These clubs were Atlanta United, San Jose Earthquakes, and Inter Miami.
  3. The total revenue generated by the league decreased by just over $24,000,000, less than 1% of the league’s actual 2025 revenue.

Conclusion

While the “Messi effect” provided an unprecedented spike in immediate revenue and media visibility, the data suggests a league that is far better insulated against his eventual departure than previous iterations after the Beckham era. With league-wide attendance remaining resilient in his absence and ever-changing roster rules now geared towards homegrown talent, MLS has shown it can flex in times of change. By learning from the financial cautionary tales of Barcelona and the commercial sustainability of PSG, and bolstered by the infrastructure of the 2026 World Cup, MLS appears less like a league facing a “doomsday” and more like one that has used its greatest catalyst to build a foundation capable of standing on its own.

CONCLUSION

MLS: At the Inflection Point | Conclusion

Thirty years ago, MLS was an experiment. Today, it is an institution, and the decisions being made right now will determine whether it becomes a global one. The evidence across these five factors points in the same direction: the league is dead set on engineering its next era. The 2026 World Cup arrives not as a lifeline but as an accelerant for a product already gaining momentum. The calendar realignment and transfer policy reforms are dismantling the structural inefficiencies that long capped the league's ceiling. The Apple TV restructuring signals confidence that the league's value will only grow, while the removal of the Season Pass paywall bets on volume over margin. The USL's pro/rel experiment, rather than posing an existential threat, is more likely to expand the overall pie by reaching markets and fan segments MLS was never built to serve. And while Messi's departure will leave a visible void, the data suggests a league far more resilient than the one that nearly folded in 2001.

The risks are real. Cold-weather fixtures will test attendance loyalty. The winter break must be filled with intention. Media fragmentation remains unresolved. Sponsorship revenue will face pressure when the league's most marketable player departs. But none of these are existential; they are the growing pains of an organization playing a longer game. The question is whether the decisions made today are ambitious enough to match the moment.

Four Key Deal Changes — Impact Assessment
1. Shortened Contract

10-year deal truncated; MLS returns to open market in 2029. A direct bet that post-Messi, post-World Cup MLS is worth more than the deal locked in 2022.

2. Higher Annual Value

$275M/yr for 2027–29 — $50M more than the previous structure would have paid. Mini-season compensated at $107.5M to account for fewer matches.

3. Paywall Removed

MLS Season Pass ($100/yr standalone) discontinued. All games now in base Apple TV+ subscription — trading direct revenue for reach and casual fan acquisition.

4. Streamlined Product

Commentators back in the broadcast booth. Spanish-language MLS 360 discontinued — 90% of the audience watched the English version, making the segment economically indefensible.

Key Insight

MLS is sacrificing 3.5 years of guaranteed revenue — precisely the 3.5 years after Lionel Messi's contract expires. A comparable 6% drop in sponsorship revenue (mirroring Ligue 1's post-Messi experience) would hurt but not destroy the league's forecast. The post-2029 media rights decision will be the most consequential business move in league history.

Sponsorship Risk

One year after Messi left France, DAZN terminated their five-year Ligue 1 broadcast agreement. Ligue 1 sponsorship revenue dropped a projected 6% in 2024/25 — followed by a 29% drop the following season. A comparable 6% drop in MLS sponsorship revenue would hurt but not destroy the forecast. The key variable: whether new deals are structured to outlast Messi's contract.

Jersey Sales

Messi ranked 2nd globally in 2025 jersey sales with 1.2M units, behind only Lamine Yamal (1.3M). At ~$130/replica with MLS capturing ~6.7% of total revenue, the league earns ~$10M/year on Messi jerseys alone — a clean line item that disappears post-departure. Combined with the estimated ~$15M/yr Apple TV subscriber share, Messi directly contributes roughly $25M/yr in identifiable MLS revenue.
